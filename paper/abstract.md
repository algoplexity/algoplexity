## **Abstract**

Collective intelligence (CI) is a fundamental yet poorly formalized phenomenon observed in multi-agent systems across biological, social, and artificial domains. Existing approaches to CI rely predominantly on statistical correlations, network structure, or data-driven models, which fail to capture the underlying generative mechanisms responsible for coordinated behavior. In this work, we propose a unified, algorithmic framework for the definition, measurement, and causal decomposition of collective intelligence grounded in principles from algorithmic information theory.

We define collective intelligence as the emergence of non-trivial compressible structure in the joint behavior of interacting agents, formalized via Kolmogorov complexity. Building on this definition, we introduce a multi-scale measurement framework comprising (i) Coordination Energy, quantifying structural redundancy, (ii) Directional Coordination, capturing temporal and causal dependence, and (iii) Information Contribution, a perturbation-based measure that attributes coordination to localized interactions. We show that collective intelligence can be decomposed into the sum of algorithmic contributions of individual interactions, enabling identification of the causal components underlying coordinated behavior.

To support empirical validation, we present a general, model-free experimental protocol applicable to synthetic systems, interaction networks, and cross-domain multi-agent settings. The proposed framework is unsupervised, substrate-independent, and grounded in first principles, providing a pathway from detection to explanation of collective intelligence. This work establishes a foundation for the systematic study of coordinated behavior as an emergent, causally decomposable property of complex systems.
